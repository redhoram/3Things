# Summarize — Short (Singkatkan)

Turn a pile of information into a message that sticks: **one core message, held up by three supporting points, in a logical order.** This is the 3Things "Rule of Three." A summary built this way is short *and* whole — the detail doesn't vanish, it just moves backstage, ready if someone asks.

## The core distinction: filter, don't cut

Cutting means shortening at random (like trimming an article mid-sentence because you ran out of room) — the result is short but broken. **Filtering** is the deliberate act of separating what's essential from what merely happens to be there, like panning for gold: throw out the sand, keep the grains that matter. Always filter.

Your compass for filtering is the **80/20 (Pareto) principle**: in almost anything, a small share of the parts carries most of the value. Keep asking: *which few points carry most of the meaning?* Find them, lead with them, and let the rest go.

## Method: the Priority Triangle

Work an inverted triangle — wide at the top, narrowing to a point:

1. **Collect everything (wide).** First, gather all the points, ideas, and data without filtering. You can't pick the best until you've seen the options.
2. **Weigh by value (middle).** Apply 80/20. Cross out the "nice to know" and keep the "need to know." You'll usually be surprised how much can go.
3. **Narrow to three, then one (point).** Keep the three most important things. Then, from those three, name the single core message — the one thing to remember if all else is forgotten.

## Required output format

Always structure the summary like this:

```
**[One-line core message — the single most important thing]**

1. [Supporting point — strongest / first in logical order]
2. [Supporting point]
3. [Supporting point]
```

Ordering rules for the three points: never leave them random. Order by importance (most → least), or chronologically, or problem → solution — whatever gives the reader a clear thread to follow.

When the user wants something even tighter (an elevator answer, a Slack blurb, a one-liner for a busy exec), also offer the **30-second / 3-sentence** form:
- **Sentence 1 — the point / conclusion** (answers the reader's silent "so what?")
- **Sentence 2 — the main reason** (so the point isn't a bare claim)
- **Sentence 3 — the action or implication** (tells them what to do with it)

Put the core message *first*, always. Treat the reader like they might "stop reading" at any moment — if the point is up front, it still lands even if they leave halfway.

## Example

Input: a 30-page quarterly performance report.

Output:
> **Revenue is up but margins are slipping — raise premium pricing next quarter.**
>
> 1. Revenue rose 12%, driven mainly by the new product line.
> 2. Margins fell because raw-material costs climbed.
> 3. Recommendation: increase prices on the premium segment next quarter.

Three-sentence version: "Revenue is up 12%, led by the new product line. Margins fell because input costs rose. Recommendation: raise premium-segment prices next quarter."

## Guardrails

- **Don't force three.** The number three is a guide for filtering, not a quota. If the material honestly has only two strong points, give two — two solid points beat three with one padded in. If it has five, don't list all five; honestly decide which three carry the most weight.
- **Simplify by organizing, not by gutting.** If a topic is genuinely complex, don't summarize away the substance. Find the "three most important doors" into the complexity as a starting handhold; the detail can follow when asked.
- **Preserve essential nuance — defer it, don't delete it.** State the clear core first, then layer nuance afterward once the foundation of understanding is built.
- **Short doesn't mean cold.** A greeting, an acknowledgment of feelings, or a bridge into the point can stay. What you trim is the excess information that buries the message, not the warmth.
- **Mastery sits behind the three.** The three points are the door you show; the full room behind them must still exist, ready to open. Know a lot; say three.
